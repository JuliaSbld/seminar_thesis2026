# Introduction

Mobile phones have become a central element of everyday life for large parts of the population, including but not limited to adolescents and young adults.[@bradley_stress_2023]
The effects of the high prominence of these mobile digital devices are highly debated among scholars as well as users.
Proposed negative effects of high usage times include a higher risk for dementia, shorter attention spans, and mental health concerns like depression or addiction.[@eikelboom_digital_2022; @neophytou_effects_2021; @sigman_screen_2017]
Yet, other scholars conducting meta-analyses of a multitude of studies investigating these effects conclude minimal or non-significant negative effects of mobile digital device usage on users' mental health.[@odgers_annual_2020; @orben_screens_2019; @bradley_stress_2023]

This field of tension can be navigated by clearly contextualizing the different positions.
Effects discussed are on the physical or mental health of users, making up entirely different fields of research.
Further, the effects can be caused by the neglect of analog activities over mobile phone usage, or by the content consumed during the usage.
Moreover, positive effects of certain usage behaviors are undermined in many discussions. [@marciano_digital_2022]
Taking these dimensions into account is necessary to steer usage behavior in a direction that benefits users.

Therefore, a more selected usage of certain digitally enabled functionalities is necessary to mitigate negative effects and foster positive ones.
Visually stimulating usage areas like short video formats or pop-up notifications can be distracting, addiction-fostering, and time-consuming for users.[@jain_exploring_2025]
Whereas, positive usage areas can entail interacting with others personally, listening to music or audiobooks, searching for information, navigation, or being guided through physical and mental exercises.[@linardon_efficacy_2024; @zhang_impact_2025]
All of these can be enabled without a need for a visual interface.

That is why this paper explores the possibility of shifting away from today's visual, screen-based interaction with mobile digital devices, bypassing the problems that come with it.
This exploration is guided by the research question:

How does a desirable future of non-visual mobile digital device interaction look, and how do we get there?

A backcasting approach has been selected to systematically answer this research question.
This entails the design of a desirable future alongside the steps that are needed to achieve said future.
To enrich both parts of the results, a student workshop has been conducted with the goal of gaining outside perspectives on the desirability of the proposed future as well as the steps needed to reach it.
Therefore, this paper offers a concrete non-visual technology design that enables positive functionalities while mitigating visual‑related harms.

The future scenario entails the widespread use of a new mobile technology consisting of an audio output and input piece (e.g. earbud or cochlear implant) and touch sensors offering the possibility of text input via the user's hand using the Lorm alphabet.
This audio and sensory interaction-based technology is a desirable alternative to today's mainly visual mobile devices, because of its more focused approach to support the everyday life of its users.

Areas needed to be considered in the implementation and roll out of this technology are the product design, the learning of lorming and the convincing of users to switch to the technology at hand.
Therefore, hardware and software advances, a widespread offering of learning tools for the Lorm alphabet, and a systematic exposure and marketing of the new technology to the public would be necessary steps to take.

Following in the next chapter is an overview of the field of tension introduced, as well as a brief explanation of the Lorm alphabet.
Chapter three gives more insights into the backcasting approach and the student workshop conducted within this research.
Results of this research are presented and discussed with respect to its implications for theoretical and practical endeavors, its limitations, and future research in chapters four and five.
The paper is concluded with a short summarization of the research.
