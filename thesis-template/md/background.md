# Theoretical Background

The increase of AI usage is prominent on all areas of daily life, influencing jobs, research and even how private communication is carried out.

## Information consumption on Social Media

The consumption of information through decentral social platforms compared to traditional information sources is highly prominent today. [source?]
Especially young adults form opinions and their perception and knowledge of the world trough content posted on Social Media.
Due to the ability of consumers to shape their experience on these platforms, information is shifting from a centrally distributed good to a highly malleable and personal good. [source?]
This shift has already been shown to promote filter bubbles in which users consume only news and information on line with their existing views. [source?]
Further, social media as a source of information is more easily instrumentalized for wrong or only partly accurate information. [source?]
Whereas centralized news platforms and journalists have have a higher standard of fact checking and accurate depictions of reality, the core of social media enables all users to distribute information, whether it is accurate or not. [source?]
Quotes, fun facts, picture and video content, health information, political statements and so much more is shared, without clear distinctions between expert knowledge and personal theories.
This flood of information rinsing over consumers of social media is forming believe systems that shape societies.[source?]

## Credibility of Messages

Trust is a sensible affected variable when a clear source of content is identifiable, for example in the contexts of traditional journalism, professional or academic contexts.
That is, because trust is affected by source, author, as well as message credibility and is formed and lost over time and not given or taken in a single moment. [source?]
In the context of Social Media, author and source credibility often take a subordinate role. [source?]
The whole platform can be perceived as the source, which is very heterogeneous and not easily given trust as a whole.
Further, authors in the form of single accounts posting on social media are often not recognized or rated by consumers.
To both, exceptions are clearly present.
Popular news institutions are present on Social Media and credited with source credibility and trust by users.
Influencers can also gain author credibility and trust by their long term followers, consuming a multitude of content by a single author.
Yet, the algorithmically assigned content by unrecognized authors for a consumer is a viable and dominant use case of consuming content.
In this case, the dominant remaining attribution to the information is message credibility.
The message itself is assigned with how accurate, authentic, and believable it is by the consumer.

## AI Use in the Context of Social Media

With the rise of Artificial Intelligence, producing content is easier than ever before. [source?] Sikorski and Hameleers
Producing text, illustrations and even video content to be posted on social platforms can happen without a human thinking about the produced content nor fact checking it.
Especially the rapid development of AI makes is increasingly difficult for consumers to judge the origin of the content they are consuming.


## AI Disclosure

This rise in AI usage in the context of Social Media raises ethical questions of whether its usage should be disclosed to consumers. [source?]
While this transparency would give consumers more information about the source of the content they are consuming, it is unclear in the current body of research how disclosing the usage of Artificial Intelligence affects users perception of content.
Several fields have been explored before, offering differing results and therefore suggesting a fragmented field of heterogenous outcomes, depending on the context and individual consuming the content.
[Chung et al and Schilke & Reimann]


## Trust in Individuals using AI

While disclosing the usage AI in professional and academic settings has been shown to diminish trust in the individual using and disclosing AI [Schilke & Reimann], disclosing it in the settings of journalism and adding more information to the disclosure showed improvements in the trust to news sources. [Chung et al]
This shows that the effects of disclosing AI usage are not clear across different areas of life and that the type of disclosure plays a relevant role in the resulting perception from consumers.
This type of disclosure can range from a dichotomous labelling of AI has been used or no label is attributed to statements with detailed information about the degree of AI usage, what parts of an output are affected and what AI has facilitated exactly.
The relevance of this degree of disclosure has been shown by Chung et al. in the context of journalism.
One type of disclosure that has comprehensively been shown to diminish trust is third party disclosure in which an outside party like a platform itself or fact checking agencies involuntarily disclose the usage of AI from a source.
Overall, the effects of self-disclosure of AI usage have been partly explored and have shown to paint different pictures across different use cases of AI usage and levels of disclosure.
Therefore, this paper explores the self-disclosure of AI usage in the field of social media.
