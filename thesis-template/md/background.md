# Theoretical Background

## Information consumption on Social Media

The consumption of information through decentralized social platforms compared to traditional information sources is highly prominent today.
Especially young adults form opinions and their perception and knowledge of the world through content posted on Social Media [@farias-batlle_young_2024].
Due to the ability of consumers to shape their experience on these platforms, information is shifting from a centrally distributed good to a highly malleable and personal good [@bhagat_curbing_2025].
This shift has already been shown to promote filter bubbles in which users consume only news and information in line with their existing views [@green_curation_2025].
Further, social media as a source of information is more easily instrumentalized for wrong or only partly accurate information [@munusamy_psychological_2024].
Whereas centralized news platforms and journalists have a higher standard of fact-checking and accurate depictions of reality, the core of social media enables all users to distribute information, whether it is accurate or not [@bhagat_curbing_2025].
Quotes, fun facts, picture and video content, health information, political statements, and so much more are shared, without clear distinctions between expert knowledge and personal theories.
This flood of information rinsing over consumers of social media is forming belief systems that shape societies [@farias-batlle_young_2024].

## AI Use in the Context of Social Media

With the rise of Artificial Intelligence, producing content is easier than ever before [@von_sikorski_introduction_2025].
Producing text, illustrations, and even video content to be posted on social platforms can happen without a human thinking about the produced content nor fact checking it.
Especially, the rapid development of AI makes it increasingly difficult for consumers to judge the origin of the content they are consuming.

### AI Disclosure

This rise in AI usage in the context of Social Media raises ethical questions of whether its usage should be disclosed to consumers [@sahebi_is_2026].
While this transparency would give consumers more information about the source of the content they are consuming, it is unclear in the current body of research how disclosing the usage of Artificial Intelligence affects users' perception of content.
Several fields have been explored before, offering differing results and therefore suggesting a fragmented field of heterogeneous outcomes, depending on the context and individual consuming the content [@chung_disclose_2026;@schilke_transparency_2025;  @baek_effect_2026; @toff_or_2025; @huschens_you_2023; @jia_news_2024; @balaban_is_2026; @kirkby_disclose_2023; @mucundorfeanu_exploring_2025].

Even within fields like advertising, research finds lowered ad credibility and attitudes towards ads on the one hand [@baek_effect_2026; @balaban_is_2026] as well as no negative effects on brand authenticity, brand attitude, and authenticity of content on the other hand  [@kirkby_disclose_2023;@mucundorfeanu_exploring_2025].

Research in the field of journalism offers some more insights into the level and kind of disclosure as relevant factors in the perceived credibility.
More detailed information on the kind of usage of AI increases the trustworthiness or credibility [@chung_disclose_2026; @jia_news_2024].
Still higher levels of AI usage and the perception of it seem to lower credibility [@jia_news_2024;@toff_or_2025] .

Yet, disclosing the usage of AI in professional and academic settings has been shown to diminish trust in the individual using and disclosing AI [@schilke_transparency_2025; @schilke_how_2026].

This shows that the effects of disclosing AI usage are not clear across different areas of life and that the type of disclosure plays a relevant role in the resulting perception from consumers.
This type of disclosure can range from a dichotomous labelling to statements with detailed information about the degree of AI usage, what parts of an output are affected, and what AI has facilitated exactly.
The relevance of this degree of disclosure has been shown by @chung_disclose_2026 in the context of journalism.
One type of disclosure that has comprehensively been shown to diminish trust is third-party disclosure, in which an outside party, like a platform itself or fact-checking agencies, involuntarily discloses the usage of AI from a source.

Overall, the effects of self-disclosure of AI usage have been partly explored and have shown to paint different pictures across different use cases of AI usage and levels of disclosure.
Based on the importance of information consumption through Social media and the differing effects of AI disclosure, this seminar paper explores the self-disclosure of AI usage in the field of social media.

## Credibility of Messages

Trust is a sensible affected variable when a clear source of content is identifiable, for example, in the contexts of traditional journalism, professional, or academic contexts [@schilke_transparency_2025].
That is, because trust is affected by source, author, as well as message credibility, and is formed and lost over time, and not given or taken in a single moment. [source?]
In the context of Social Media, author and source credibility often take a subordinate role. [source?]
The whole platform can be perceived as the source, which is very heterogeneous and not easily given trust as a whole.
Further, authors in the form of single accounts posting on social media are often not recognized or rated by consumers.
To both, exceptions are clearly present.
Popular news institutions are present on Social Media and are credited with source credibility and trust by users.
Influencers can also gain author credibility and trust from their long-term followers, consuming a multitude of content by a single author.
Yet, the algorithmically assigned content by unrecognized authors for a consumer is a viable and dominant use case of consuming content.
In this case, the dominant remaining attribution to the information is message credibility.
The message itself is assigned with how accurate, authentic, and believable it is by the consumer [@appelman_measuring_2016].

That is why message credibility is used as the dependent variable in this research, whereas AI disclosure is used as the independent variable.
Based on the presented research, three levels of AI disclosure are used within this seminar paper.
Those are (1) no AI disclosure is present, (2) Partial AI Usage is indicated, and (3) full AI generation of the content is communicated.
When using no AI disclosure as the baseline, the effect of (2) and (3) on message credibility results in the following hypotheses.

H1a: Partial AI disclosure leads to higher message credibility compared to no AI disclosure.
H1b: Full AI disclosure leads to lower message credibility compared to no AI disclosure.

Based on the findings of @chung_disclose_2026, the more nuanced AI disclosure is anticipated to make the message of the marked social media post more credible due to higher transparency perception.
Whereas the disclosure of fully AI-generated content should follow the literary consensus of lower credibility ratings when AI involvement is indicated dichotomously.

## Effect of AI disclosure on perceived AI Usage

@jia_news_2024 indicated in their research that not the labeling of AI involvement, but rather the perception of this involvement, is decisive to lower credibility ratings of content.
This mediating role of AI perception, between AI disclosure and message credibility, is therefore included within this research and leads to the following hypotheses.

H2a: No AI disclosure leads to below-average perceived AI usage.
H2b: Partial AI disclosure leads to above-average perceived AI usage.
H2c: Full AI disclosure leads to above-average perceived AI usage.

## Effect of Perceived AI Usage on Message Credibility

In line with introducing AI perception as a possible mediator and the consensus of the majority of consulted literature, the effect of higher AI perception is hypothesized to lower the message credibility of content.

H3: Higher AI perception leads to lower message credibility of content.

## Personal Features as possible moderators

An under-investigated area in the field of AI disclosure is personal factors, which might moderate the overall effects discussed before.
Therefore, two variables are added to the research model, giving more insights into differences between social media users.
First, critical media literacy, as the “abilities to analyze and judge media messages and understand them at various contextual levels” [@koc_development_2016], is taken into account.
Secondly, the personal attitude towards AI is considered [@grassini_development_2023].

### AI Attitude

The evaluation of AI-generated content as (non-)credible might be influenced by the attitude an individual holds towards the abilities of AI.
More positive views towards the content AI is producing might influence the effect of perceived AI usage on message credibility in a positive way.
Whereas negative views towards AI might lower message credibility ratings, because the content is not evaluated based on its message credibility but rather on perceived AI Usage.

H5: A more positive AI attitude leads to higher message credibility ratings despite high perceptions of AI Usage.
