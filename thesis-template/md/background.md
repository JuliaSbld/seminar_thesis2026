# Theoretical Background

In order to motivate the following backcasting approach and the future scenario presented, a presentation of the current state of discussion of technology usage, as well as an introduction to the Lorm alphabet, is necessary.

## Technology Usage

The interaction with screen-based mobile devices (e.g., smartphones) is a dominant aspect of today's technology usage.

The effects of high usage times of mobile devices, especially including social media, are a field of tension within the literature.
While some scholars present detrimental effects on the physical and mental well-being of users with high usage times [@eikelboom_digital_2022], others argue little to no measurable effects across studies [@odgers_annual_2020; @orben_screens_2019].
It is to be noted here that the topic is investigated from different fields, mainly including psychology and neuroscience.

Neuroscience scholars presented a multitude of negative effects stemming from Internet use disorders.
These include alterations of brain matter, suggesting higher rates in early-stage dementia from excessive screen exposure [@eikelboom_digital_2022; @neophytou_effects_2021].
Further, lowered self-esteem, increases in mental health issues and addiction, as well as slower learning and acquisition, are suggested in adolescents and young adults [@neophytou_effects_2021].
The finding of increases in addiction is supported when taking into account that video game playing is associated with a similar magnitude of dopamine release as drug usage [@weinstein_neurobiological_2020].

The argument of differing usage of mobile devices adds an important depth to the discussion [@marciano_digital_2022].
Even within one platform, e.g. Instagram, TikTok, or YouTube, the content consumed by users can vary to a great magnitude.
While some content might raise feelings of fear of missing out, heightened isolation, or lower self-esteem, other content can lower feelings of stress and loneliness.
Further, one-to-one communication as well as communities enabled by digital devices offer a way to gain the positive effects of human interaction in an online setting [@marciano_digital_2022].

Overall, the negative effects of certain usage and content exposure are important to be aware of, yet neutral or positive effects can not be left unconsidered.
A promotion of content, interaction, and usage of digital devices promoting these positive effects, while lowering exposure and overusage of harmful content and usage patterns, is a sensible approach to move within this field of scholarly tension.

## Non-visual Technology Usage

Many digital devices used today rely heavily on visual stimuli conveyed on screens.

This focus can be questioned when considering arguments for more tactile interactions on the well-being of consumers.
@barrios-oneill_exploring_2022 argue that the high tactile sensibility of our hands is in need of stimulation to promote well-being.
Some tactile stimulations can, for example, improve states of mindfulness and attention [@stanko-kaczmarek_effects_2016].
Yet, screens offer little tactile stimulation and variety.
Moreover, an over-representation of vision compared to other sensory modalities is present in other areas of research [@hutmacher_why_2019].
When considering this focus on vision-based interaction, as well as the missing positive effects of tactile stimulation, the question of alternative technology interaction can be raised.

Non-visual use cases entail listening to music, which has been shown to have positive effects on the subjective well-being and sleep quality of individuals [@zhao_systematic_2024; @zhang_impact_2025].
Also, mindfulness applications, guiding through meditation and other mental exercises, have positive effects on the mood, stress levels, and attentional control of users [@walsh_effects_2019].
Further, one-to-one communication with mutual (online) friendships can help to reduce feelings of loneliness and stress [@marciano_digital_2022].

These considerations show the possibility of selecting and promoting usage behaviors benefitting the mental health of users.


## Lorm Alphabet

The Lorm alphabet is a system of mapping the letters of the latin alphabet to the hand.
Due to its origin in Austria, the main group of users are German-speaking.
It uses touch motions to communicate these letters, which in the following will be referred to as lorming.
For that, the hand of a usually deaf-blind person is touched by a communication partner.
Gestures like tapping and stroking different areas of the hand correspond to different letters, resulting in building sentences letter-by-letter (see Figure \ref{fig:1.1}) [@schmidt_zoning-based_2016].

![Lorm Alphabet, [@WinterbergTrueType] \label{fig:1.1}](figs/Lormalphabet.svg){width=40%}

This is a relevant difference to sign language used by deaf individuals, which is not a letter by letter translation of the spoken language.
Therefore, the Lorm alphabet can be viewed closer to a means of input than its own language.

### Learning

This difference is mainly important for the learning and application possibilities of lorming.
While the language most spoken using the Lorm alphabet is German, using it to convey other languages that use the Latin alphabet in writing is possible.
When learning to lorm, only the position of corresponding letters in the hand, combined with the assigned motion of touch, needs to be learned.

Attempts like the LeLorm App show the feasibility of designing a learning aid of the alphabet as well as a positive user study of sighted and hearing individuals with no prior knowledge of lorming.
In less than 30 minutes, three participants were able to learn corresponding motions to the letters of the alphabet with accuracies above 95% [@schmidt_zoning-based_2016].

### Usage

As a means of communication, the Lorm alphabet is used by deaf-blind individuals as well as caretakers, family members, or translators.
Due to the focus of users of the Lorm alphabet in German-speaking countries and the relatively small number of deaf-blind individuals, it is rather unknown.
The inability of hearing and sighted individuals to communicate with deaf-blind individuals leads to the isolation and exclusion of these individuals from many parts of society [@hersh_deafblind_2013].

Overall, it can be noted that basic knowledge of the Lorm alphabet is feasible to acquire for hearing and sighted individuals, and the knowledge of such could bring great benefits for deaf-blind individuals.

While the current state of mobile technology usage and its effects are prominently discussed in IS, psychology, and neuroscience literature, no concrete IS design artifacts offer alternative future directions.
To navigate the effects identified, this paper addresses this gap using a backcasting approach.
