The selection and focus on certain functionalities within this paper should inspire others to take a deeper look at the effects of different kinds of mobile technology usage and its implications.

## Implications for the Practice

Designing a new possibility if technology interaction within this paper is a great opportunity for users as well as designers of mobile digital technologies to rethink current design and usage of smartphones.
Offering new solutions to the overusage and overstimulation of users through fast paste content offerings has the opportunity of harvesting positive effects of mobile digital technologies while reducing negative implications.
Taking out the speed in digital content in favor of a more calm, mindful and intellectually stimulating experience of content consumption is a route that should be explored more.
The approach presented in this paper is possibility for bringing this vision into existence, yet it is not the only possibility to approach the field of tension which inspired it.


## Limitations

There are several limitations faced by this paper and its methodology.

First of all, the normative nature of the backcasting approach, while being addressed by the student workshop is still a subjective endeavour.
The desirability can be questioned in several aspects, including the focus on auditory communication and the inclusion of the lorm alphabet.
Yet, this lays in the nature of the approach.

Another limitation is the small research base regarding the learning of the lorm alphabet for hearing and sighted individuals without contact to a deaf-blind person.
While I hypothesize the feasibility of learning lorming in a reasonable timeframe based on @schmidt_zoning-based_2016, it remains unclear if that is correct, especially when aiming for a very fluent usage of the lorm alphabet.

Furthermore, the potential user involvement in form of the student workshop faces limitations in time and amount of users.
Bigger group sizes, longer discussion times or several rounds of user involvement could bring up more aspects relevant to this research.
Including other age groups or individuals with different technological literacies would also have enriched the feedback.
A very interesting group to be included would have been blind individuals, for which tactile and auditive interaction is already more present in their technology usage and everyday lives.

Overall, this backcasting approach could present a clearer picture if more diverse stakeholders would have been included and more information on the learning of lorming would be available.


## Future Research

Several directions of action can be taken from this paper.

Grounded in the small population of lorm alphabet users, the research regarding learning it is slim.
@schmidt_zoning-based_2016 laid some groundwork here, yet the user study conducted is on a very small scale.
Especially for sighted and hearing individuals, a systematic study of duration, and difficulties of learning to use the lorm alphabet would be of great value to the thoughts presented in this paper.
That is, because the ease of learning to lorm is an important factor in the ease of using the entire proposed technology.

Furthermore, an interesting endeavour could be a prototyping study, trying to use technologies available today to make a physical prototype of the proposed technology.
Building on existing smart gloves [@ozioko_smart_2022] or other promising tactile sensors [@pyo_recent_2021] offers realistic chances for the input part of the prototype.
The auditive output is in my assessment also imitable with available headphones connected to a device running a software prototype of the screenless interface.
The availability of such a prototype would give a much more tangible option to find flaws and opportunities of the idea compared to a purely theoretical model.

Another field in need of a closer investigation is the impact of auditory technologies on areas negatively impacted by their visual counterparts.
Due to the high preference of discussing vision compared to other sensory modalities [@hutmacher_why_2019], as well as the high occurrence of visual interaction in digital technologies (?) this focus on auditory technologies is underrepresented.
A systematic comparison between effects of visual technologies and auditory technologies would give a more stable base for the question of which route is more beneficial on the mental and physical health of users.
