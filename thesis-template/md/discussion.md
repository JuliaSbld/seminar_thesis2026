# Discussion

## Implications for the Theory

This paper advances IS research through two contributions.
Firstly, a novel audio-haptic mobile technology design is introduced, which preserves beneficial functionalities for its users while eliminating 'screen-time'.
This design offers a new perspective to the discussion of mobile technology's effects on users.

Secondly, using a second generation backcasting approach to address a human computer interaction issue extends classic use cases of this method, which usually focuses on sustainability issues in our society. [@robinson_future_2003; @quist_participatory_2006; @dreborg_essence_1996]
The stakeholder workshop prioritizes user acceptance of the scenario over technical feasibility, embodying the fundamental idea of the methodology in this new context.

## Implications for the Practice

Considering new ways of technology interaction is a great opportunity for mobile technology developers to rethink the current design and usage of smartphones.
Offering new solutions to the overusage and overstimulation of users through fast-paced content offerings has the opportunity of harvesting positive effects of mobile digital technologies while reducing negative implications.
Taking out the speed in digital content in favor of a more calm, mindful, and intellectually stimulating experience of content consumption is a route that should be explored more.
Technology developers can operationalize this vision through iterative prototyping of earbud-glove interfaces using existing components [@ozioko_smart_2022], followed by lorming learning applications with haptic feedback.
Targeting users concerned about screen time offers immediate market positioning, as the design maintains compatibility with dominant communication and music platforms, mitigating network effect barriers.

## Limitations

There are several limitations faced by this paper and its methodology.

First of all, the normative nature of the backcasting approach, while being addressed by the student workshop, is still a subjective endeavor.
The desirability can be questioned in several aspects, including the focus on auditory communication and the inclusion of the Lorm alphabet.
Yet, this lies in the nature of the approach.
Additionally, findings from information systems students may reflect domain-specific perspectives rather than broader demographic preferences.

Another limitation is the small research base regarding the learning of the Lorm alphabet for hearing and sighted individuals without contact with a deaf-blind person.
While I hypothesize the feasibility of learning lorming in a reasonable timeframe based on @schmidt_zoning-based_2016, it remains unclear if that is correct, especially when aiming for a very fluent usage of the lorm alphabet.

Furthermore, the potential user involvement in the form of the student workshop faces limitations in time and the number of users.
Bigger group sizes, longer discussion times, or several rounds of user involvement could bring up more aspects relevant to this research.
Including other age groups or individuals with different technological literacies would also have enriched the feedback.
A very interesting group to be included would have been blind individuals, for whom tactile and auditory interaction is already more present in their technology usage and everyday lives.

Overall, this backcasting approach could present a clearer picture if more diverse stakeholders had been included and more information on the learning of lorming were available.


## Future Research

Several directions of action can be taken from this paper.

Grounded in the small population of Lorm alphabet users, the research regarding learning it is slim.
@schmidt_zoning-based_2016 laid some groundwork here, which showed 95% letter recognition accuracy after 30 minutes (n=3 sighted/hearing participants).
Yet, the user study conducted is on a very small scale.
Especially for sighted and hearing individuals, a systematic study of the duration and difficulties of learning to use the Lorm alphabet would be of great value to the thoughts presented in this paper.
That is, because the ease of learning to lorm is an important factor in the ease of using the entire proposed technology.

Furthermore, an interesting endeavour could be a prototyping study, trying to use technologies available today to make a physical prototype of the proposed technology.
Building on existing smart gloves [@ozioko_smart_2022] or other promising tactile sensors [@pyo_recent_2021] offers realistic chances for the input part of the prototype.
The auditory output is, in my assessment, also imitable with available headphones connected to a device running a software prototype of the screenless interface.
The availability of such a prototype would give a much more tangible option to find flaws and opportunities in the idea compared to a purely theoretical model.

Another field in need of closer investigation is the impact of auditory technologies on areas negatively impacted by their visual counterparts.
Due to the high preference for discussing vision compared to other sensory modalities a focus on auditory technologies is underrepresented. [@hutmacher_why_2019]
A systematic comparison between the effects of visual technologies and auditory technologies would give a more stable base for the question of which route is more beneficial for the mental and physical health of users.
