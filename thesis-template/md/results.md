# Results

The final goal within fifty years is the predominant use of audio-focused mobile technology.
This technology, in its final form, entails an implant that is able to stimulate the audio reception of users, as well as implanted sensors in one hand, which are able to detect touch.
Users can interact with the device with spoken word or lorming, which is detected via the hand sensors.
Artificial Intelligence enables natural-feeling interaction with the device, which is able to carry out a multitude of functionalities currently enabled by smartphones.
Examples are receiving and sending messages and calls, searching for information, listening to music, audiobooks, podcasts, and other audio input formats, as well as setting alarms and reminders.
Taken together, this scenario condenses the theoretical considerations on non‑visual interaction into a single coherent future vision for mobile technology and forms the reference point for assessing desirability and implementation steps in the workshop.

## Desirability

Considering the normative nature of the backcasting approach used, examining the perceived desirability of the proposed future scenario is necessary.
During the student workshop, interesting perspectives on this matter have been collected.
The workshop thereby functions not as a representative evaluation, but as a qualitative plausibility check of the desirability assumptions underlying the backcasting scenario.
Overall, 75% of workshop participants stated they perceived the proposed technology as desirable.
This majority indicates that, at least among informed students, the core idea is intuitively acceptable rather than fundamentally rejected.

Positive aspects of the technology identified during this research entail a more mindful interaction with technology, high accessibility, and compatibility with existing technologies.
The first aspect is mainly enabled by fewer distractions when interacting with the technology compared to a smartphone.
Visual cues on the smartphone, like notifications and app symbols, do not exist in the proposed technology, reducing chronic sensory stimulation, which is argued to increase the risk of several mental disorders [@eikelboom_digital_2022].
In addition, mental health enablers like guided breathing, meditation exercises and music therapy are still enabled by the technology [@linardon_efficacy_2024; @zhao_systematic_2024].
These arguments indicate that perceived desirability is primarily grounded in expectations of reduced cognitive overload and more mindful technology use, rather than in increased efficiency or feature richness.

Another desirable aspect, which has been identified by workshop participants is the accessibility of the technology with respect to blind and potentially also deaf-blind users.
The lack of a visual component makes sight irrelevant for using the technology and an impulses given by the hand sensors could substitute the audio aspect of the technology [@pantera_feel_2025].
Furthermore, cochlear implants can be used as the means of audio output and have been shown to be helpful to deaf-blind users as well  [@daneshi_outcome_2022].
Aside from the accessibility of the technology, the societal implications could be of much greater value to deaf and deaf-blind individuals.
Due to the new incentive for able-bodied individuals to learn the Lorm alphabet the interaction with deaf and deaf-blind individuals would become possible for many more people.
Therefore, the proposed future would not only unlock a new way of technology interaction, but also reduce communication barriers and the isolation of deaf-blind individuals [@hersh_deafblind_2013].
In sum, the scenario combines individual-level benefits with collective benefits in terms of accessibility and social inclusion, which reinforces its normative desirability.

Nonetheless, negative aspects of the technology must also be taken into consideration when discussing the desirability of the proposed future.
The two main arguments made by workshop participants are the steep learning curve to use the technology, as well as slower interaction with the technology compared to smartphones.
The steep learning curve stemming from learning the Lorm alphabet is a considerable hindering factor for the adoption of the technology and therefore needs to be closely monitored when introducing it.

Further, the slower interaction is based on longer speaking times compared to the reading times of text, which affects the auditory output for presenting information.
While this is a sensible critique of the interaction, slowing down digital interaction is also one of the benefits and objectives of the technology, making it a calculated risk.

When taking all aspects into account, the desirability of the scenario presented is relatively high, especially for users who actively seek more mindful and less visually stimulating technology use.
Yet, a central takeaway is that a screenless technology is unlikely to serve as a one‑to‑one replacement for all mobile phone users, but rather as a complementary alternative for specific user segments.

## Steps to be taken

Achieving the scenario relies on several dimensions of change, which should all be aided by a specific roadmap, while recognizing their interdependencies.
This structuring already reflects the workshop finding that technological, skill-related, and societal factors must co‑evolve for the scenario to become reality, rather than any single dimension being sufficient on its own.

### Product Design

To address the necessary achievements in functionalities and product design, the proposed technology can be separated into four parts.
First, there is the audio output and input.
An immediately available example of this part is earbuds, currently used in connection with mostly mobile devices.
When considering these models for the proposed technology, an aspect to be improved is the battery life.
Current models have a working time of up to 8 hours before needing to be charged, which would be a problem for continuous use in everyday life [@apple_airpods_2025].
Another technology to be considered in the future could be implants.
Cochlear implants, which are used today to electrically stimulate the auditory nerve of individuals with hearing loss, would be a viable option here [@sutton_cochlear_2025].

Secondly, the software supporting the technology has to be developed.
This includes connections to use existing applications using the input of voice and lorming as well as developing an overall structure for the screenless interaction.
Natural responses when seeking information, reading messages, and navigating the technology are enabled by current advances in LLMs, which can be considered sufficient for the interaction needed.

When discussing the third part, the hardware providing the computing power for the device, their physical size should be considered.
Hardware offering the needed computing power are currently too big for earbuds or implants [@marco_chiappetta_inside_2025].
Yet, based in Moore's Law, an ongoing reduction in chip sizes is realistic, offering a clear vision of having small enough hardware to support the computing power needed [@moore_cramming_1965].

The last part are the sensors, which detect the tactile input of the user.
Current research already suggests gloves, which are able to detect inputs of the Lorm alphabet [@ozioko_smart_2022; @luo_adaptive_2024].
Other options for flexible tactile sensors can also be explored when considering designing less visually noticeable options [@pyo_recent_2021].

Overall, current technologies already offer a very strong basis needed for the development of the proposed technology.
This is also reflected within the student workshop, in which aspects of product design are mentioned in three out of four answers to the question of which steps are required to implement the idea.
Yet, when asking for the biggest challenge, no responses mention product design aspects.
This pattern indicates that product design is perceived as a largely solvable problem, both by current technology trends and by the informed workshop participants.
For the roadmap, this means that product design activities can largely follow existing technological trajectories, while other dimensions require more active steering.

### Learning Lorming

In order to use the technology to its full potential, lorming is a key aspect to ensure a fast and non-intrusive means of input.

The process of learning can be divided into getting familiar with the structure, including remembering the mapping of letters to hand regions, and gestures by heart and practicing the usage of this mapping.
Tools like learning apps for the Lorm alphabet can enable this learning, especially in the early stages [@schmidt_zoning-based_2016].
Based on @schmidt_zoning-based_2016, a single session of familiarization with the alphabet can be enough to internalize the mapping.
The bigger challenge is the longer practice period needed to be able to use lorming in a way that is a substitute for the spoken language.
This indicates that the main difficulty is not initial familiarization, but achieving a level of fluency that makes lorming a realistic everyday input method.

Looking at the technology proposal at hand, building in a learning tool that gives auditory feedback about the correctness of input via the hand sensors is a sensible approach.
Because the technology is not entirely dependent on lorming, learning lorming with the help of the technology is plausible.

Within the overall backcasting pathway, learning lorming therefore emerges as a key individual-level challenge that must be addressed through systematic training support.
This positions learning lorming as a medium‑risk challenge that can be mitigated through technology‑integrated training, but requires sustained individual commitment for widespread adoption.

### Convincing Users

'The only thing actually missing is the acceptance of society.'

Convincing users to use the new technology has been identified as the key challenge by all workshop participants.
Current mobile phone usage is prevalent in a way in which it is hard to imagine a reduction in it.
Benefits of the proposed technology are connected to an entirely different way of interacting with this device, which is arguably hard to motivate for many users.
Therefore, a structured marketing approach would be needed to bring the technology closer to its users.
A relevant benefit of the design here is the compatibility with today's applications used on mobile phones.
The auditory and haptic interaction is, for example, possible when using messengers (e.g. WhatsApp), music applications (e.g. Spotify), or meditation options (e.g. Headspace).
This reduces network effects, hindering early adopters from using the new technology.
Nonetheless, a clear communication of its benefits needs to be prioritized.
Targeting mobile phone users who want to reduce their screen time and practice more mindful usage practices of technology and social media is the key to introducing the proposed alternative.

Focusing this group gives the technology the opportunity to rise as a niche product.
Additionally, improvements in the previously mentioned areas can aid users to switch away from mobile phones.
The goal of reaching a widespread user base in a few decades is plausible if the trend of screen time reduction continues in society as a whole.
Nonetheless, positive effects of the non-visual technology can be present for any size of user base, not being dependent on a certain threshold.
Taken together, the workshop feedback positions societal acceptance and behavior change as the primary bottleneck in realizing the proposed future, making user convincing the central strategic step in the roadmap.

Across all dimensions, the workshop input confirms the scenario’s technical plausibility while highlighting user acceptance and skill acquisition as the critical path dependencies for backcasting success.
These steps form an interdependent roadmap that prioritizes societal and behavioral change over purely technological advancement.
