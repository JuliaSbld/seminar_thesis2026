# Results

## Effects of AI disclosure on Message Credibility

To explore the overarching influence of AI disclosure an message credibility their connection is being explored using a mixed effects model.
On the one hand, results show no significant influence of partial AI disclosure on message credibility (β = +0.199, p = .425).
On the other hand, the influence of full AI disclosure is significant and shows a substantial effect ( β = -1.547, p < .001).
This shows a noticeable difference between the different levels of AI disclosure, meaning that AI disclosure cannot be viewed as a dichotomous variable, but is in need of more precise definition, even in the context of short and informal information sharing like on Social Media.
The group variance of reported levels of message credibility is 0.917, indicating the relevance of the random intercept, which has been used exactly for the purpose of taking these between subject differences of overall message credibility perception into account.

## Levels of AI Disclosure And Perceived AI Usage

To establish a baseline of perceived AI usage, the posts without disclosure were reported to be perceived below the midpoint of the scale (M = 3.09, SD = 1.77) significantly.
A t-test against the midpoint (4.0) shows this (t = -3,901, p < .001) clearly, indicating participants perceived non disclosed to be predominantly human made.
The hypothesis of lower perceived AI usage, when no disclosure is present is therefore supported by my dataset.

Partial AI disclosure shows a higher perception of AI usage (M = 5.37, SD = 1.34), testing against the midpoint shows a significant leaning towards perceiving the Post as AI made (t = 6.276, p < .001).
The hypothesized positive connection of partial AI disclosure on perceived AI usage is also supported by this finding.

Full disclosure behaves very in line with this trend, perception rises towards AI creation (M = 6.03, SD = 1.33).
With on average only one point off from entirely AI created it is supported to suggest a high perceived AI usage (t = 9.426, p < .001).
This strong positive effect of full AI disclosure on perceived AI usage is in line with the hypothesis in my model.

Overall, the hypotheses regarding the connections of AI disclosure and perceived AI usage are supported by my data very clearly.

## Perceived AI Usage and Message Credibility

The linear mixed model shows a significant negative effect of perceived AI Usage on message credibility (β = −0.30, p < .001, 95% CI [−0.42, −0.19])).
Therefore, the hypothesis for the b path og my model is supported and higher perceiption of AI usage lower the message credibility of the stimuli.

## CMC and perceived AI usage

To test critical media literacy as a moderator of the connections between AI disclosure levels and perceived AI usage a linear mixed model is used again to account for baseline variance of participants.
When no disclosure is present, higher CML shows to be significantly lowering the perceived AI usage of the posts (β = −0.645, p = .014).
Therefore, participants with higher critical media literacy are perceiving posts with no disclosure as less likely to be created with AI.
Partly disclosing AI involvement leads to higher perceived AI usage in higher CML participants (β = +0.872, p = .019), meaning higher critical media literacy lets participants react more strongly to partial disclosure by indicating higher perceived AI usage.
Full disclosure results of CML moderating the connection are not significant, but the trend is still in line with the effect of partial disclosure.
Participants perceive more AI usage in full disclosure context, when scoring higher on CML (β = +0.607, p = .143).

These findings contradict the hypothesis of higher CML leading to being less influenced by the disclosure level of the stimulus.
The results in fact show the exact opposite with participants with higher CML rather amplifying the disclosure level in their perception of AI usage.

## Perceived AI x Attitude Moderation

Testing the hypothesis of wether attitude towards AI moderates the negative effect of perceived AI usage on message credibility shows no significant results (β = +0.011, p = .804).
Therefore, neither a more positive view of AI results in keeping higher message credibility scores despite higher perceived AI usage nor more negative view of AI results in lowering the message credibility even further when perceiving higher AI usage.
Even tough the hypothesis is not supported in my data, the small n = 19 of this research could influence this result and would be interesting to test again with a larger dataset.

## Mediation

The proposed research model introduces perceived AI usage as a mediator between AI disclosure and message credibility.
This proposition is analyzed using OLS regression for partial and full disclosure in contrast to no disclosure and shows heterogenous results.

The direct effect of partial disclosure on message credibility is strongly positive (β = +1.294, 95% CI [+0.773, +1.840], p < .001), indicating higher credibility when partial AI disclosure is present.
Yet, the indirect effect of partial disclosure is negative through increasing AI perception which lowers message credibility (β = −0.461, 95% CI [−0.888, −0.095], p = .016).
Overall, the effect analyzed through the OLS regression is still positive (β = +0.832, 95% CI [+0.170, +1.502], p = .012).
These results suggest inconsistent mediation through perceived AI Usage.
Even tough partial AI disclosure increases message credibility, AI perception is also increased, which then lowers the direct positive effect on message credibility.
The perception of AI usage suppresses the positive effect partially, that would otherwise result from the disclosure.

For full disclosure the effects are more consistent.
A direct negative effect can be noted (β = −1.118, 95% CI [−1.691, −0.553], p < .001) as well as an indirect negative effect (β = −0.503, 95% CI [−0.864, −0.192], p < .001).
This results in a strong negative total effect of full AI disclosure on message credibility (β = −1.621, 95% CI [−2.162, −1.074], p < .001).
The effect is partially mediated through perceived AI Usage, with 31% of the total effect being mediated and 69% remaining as a direct effect of the disclosure without considering the effect it has on perceived AI Usage.

## Demographics

Demographic characteristics are examined as possibly directly influencing perceived AI usage or message credibility.
Age and gender were tested in this regard on these variables in a mixed effects model, while controlling for the disclosure level present in the posts.
Perceived AI usage is neither significantly predicted by age (β = −0.01, p = .590) nor gender (β = −0.04, p = .908).
This indicates that the perception of AI involvement was driven by AI disclosure rather than participant characteristics.
This is supported by the results showing that both partial (β = +2.28, p < .001) and full disclosure (β = +2.94, p < .001) substantially raise perceived AI Usage relative to no disclosure.
The level of AI disclosure is therefore the dominant predictor of perceived AI usage in my sample.
Similarly, age (β = +0.007, p = .776) and gender (β = +0.346, p = .517) also did not influence the perception of message credibility significantly.
These findings suggest that the mediation chain operates consistently across demographic differences within my sample.
