# Conclusion

This paper discusses a potential future of auditory mobile technologies to overcome the negative impacts of today's visual mobile technology usage, while still enabling functionalities seen as having primarily positive effects.

A backcasting approach is used to formulate a desirable future of the widespread use of mainly auditory and haptic mobile technology interaction.
Further, the steps needed to achieve this future are discussed.
Both parts of the backcasting approach are enriched by the opinions gathered within a student workshop, consisting of five university students in the field of information systems.

Results show a moderately high desirability of the proposed future, which ultimately consists of an earpiece receiving and sending auditory signals from and to the user, as well as a set of hand sensors receiving and processing signals of Lorm alphabet input on the hand of the user.
Further, the main areas of steps needed to be taken to achieve said future have been found to be the technical improvement of the technology's components, the widespread learning of the Lorm alphabet, as well as convincing potential users to integrate the technology into their daily lives.

The technology envisioned within this research positions itself as a possible solution for promoting a more mindful and less intrusive way of integrating digital technologies into our daily lives.
Yet, the field of tension this solution is based upon is still in need of more theoretical contributions, discussing not only if but which applications, usage patterns, or content have a positive or negative effect on users' mental and physical health.
Even though a purely auditory and haptic technology is far away from the mobile technology usage today, developers should reflect upon the possibility of less visually (over-)stimulating applications to foster reducing screen time.
