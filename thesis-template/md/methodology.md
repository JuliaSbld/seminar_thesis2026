# Methodology

## Backcasting

### General Motivation

Backcasting is a normative future study approach with the goal of designing a desirable future and formulating steps to achieve this future.
The term is shaped by @robinson_futures_1990, who offers the approach as an alternative to forecasting approaches, which are meant to predict the most likely future based on the current trajectory of an area.
The focus on desirability of the designed future is linked to a rather long time span in the future, typically 25 to 50 years are chosen. [@robinson_futures_1990]

While precise approaches differ in concrete studies, the main objectives of the backcasting approach remain.
One or multiple desirable future scenarios are laid out, which are followed by formulating steps for stakeholders to be taken in order to achieve the desired future.
These stakeholders include governmental institutions, political parties, the general public, companies, and private organizations. [@dreborg_essence_1996]

Ideas used to design the desired future can be generated in different approaches, brainstorming, expert panels, and stakeholder involvement are just to name a few. [@robinson_future_2003; @dreborg_essence_1996; @quist_past_2006]
The involvement of stakeholders can be highlighted here as an important factor for the so-called 'second generation' of backcasting. [@robinson_future_2003]

Overall, backcasting offers the great ability to detach a future study from the current state of the world, making it possible to generate entirely new ideas as opposed to following the rails of today's way of life.[@dreborg_essence_1996]

### Application in this Paper

That is why this approach has been chosen to address the research question proposed in this paper.
The problem of balancing the positive effects of mobile digital devices while reducing the negative effects on the mental and physical health of individuals can benefit from the discussed detachment from the current state of smartphone usage.
Further, the time span for implementing the proposed future can realistically be placed in up to 50 years.

Motivated by the problems of today's usage of mobile digital devices [@eikelboom_digital_2022; @tu_effects_2025], positive and negative use cases of these devices have been identified.
Positive use cases included digitally enabled human interactions [@marciano_digital_2022], music [@zhang_impact_2025], physical exercise guidance [@hu_examining_2023], and the consumption of information, including, for example, audiobooks, podcasts, and news [@carrotte_impact_2025].
The common denominator of these use cases has been found to be the missing need for a visual interface to enable them.
Combined with the considerations of @barrios-oneill_exploring_2022 as well as @reed_haptic_2023, the input and output means of the desired mobile technology have been set.
With auditory input and output enriched by tactile input via the Lorm alphabet, the idea for a technology enabling positive use cases of digital mobile devices without using a visual interface was found.

Inspired by @robinson_future_2003 and @quist_past_2006, the following steps of the approach have been enriched by involving informed stakeholders, which took place in the form of a student workshop.

## Student Workshop

The student workshop took place in early December 2025 and included five students enrolled in bachelor's and master's programs in the field of information systems.
It should be noted that this number of workshop participants (n=5) is not generalizable, but able to inform the normative backcasting scenario.

Involving students as a means of collecting input is motivated by several factors.
First, the age group of university students today presents a high usage of digital technologies, including social media [@odgers_annual_2020], exposing them to possible problematic usage of such technologies.
Further, the timeline for theoretically achieving the proposed future is still relevant to them, making them potential users of the technology in the future.
At last, the selection of information systems students provided the workshop with informed participants, who had prior knowledge of digital technologies and the effects of such technologies in economic and societal scenarios.

The main objective of the student workshop was to gather informed opinions about (1) the desirability of the designed future and (2) the steps needed to achieve it.
In order to achieve this, the time slot of 45 minutes available for the workshop has been split into three parts.

The first six minutes have been used to inform the participants about the identified problem and present the previously generated idea of a new mobile digital technology.
Possible use cases of the technology have been mentioned, and the Lorm alphabet has been introduced to the participants.

Following this introduction, the participants have been asked to answer six questions, four of which cover the desirability aspect and two cover the steps needed.
Answers have been collected using a digital tool, which took the participants about 17 minutes, including short introductions to each question.

The second half of the workshop was used to facilitate a discussion about the topic, gathering more ideas and opinions that could not have been anticipated with static questions.
Giving the participants the room to voice their opinions and being able to ask follow-up questions gave the workshop the depth needed to critically assess the desirability of the proposed future.
